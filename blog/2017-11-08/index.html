Bit and byte
ad
Nov 8, 2017
2 minutes read

In the computer science area, it is quite common to implement bit or byte for faster calculation. It is known that each bit could represent 2 states eg. 0 or 1. Therefore, in order to represent a hexadecimal, we need to have 4 bits to do that(2 x 2 x 2 x 2). Hence, a hexadecimal should contain 4 bits.

1100 is an example of 4 bits that represent 12 in decimal while C in hexadecimal.

You may wonder why I talk about this? Here is the point, in the programming world, since 0 and 1 are the basic 2 states that could represent a value. Therefore, any notation that based on the power of 2 could be more straight forward to understand for a computer. Like 8, 16, 32, 64 …

When it comes to the area of encoding or network communication, data is transferred in the form of byte array. (I have no idea why it is in this way). Basically the length of each element in the array is 2 bytes which is 16 bits. What I want to say here is that you can’t really tell from the result of the byte array of how long it is for each element. It is represented in hexadecimal which means you will get 16 * 16 states which also mean 8 bits for each element.

What a twisted world it is for a computer to think. But wait, maybe it is strange for a computer to understand your way of thinking. Either way, once you get comfortable with thinking in this way, a lot of things start to make sense.
